How the behavior of an organism comes together

Behavior analysis has uncovered dozens of behavioral principles. We study how those principles and processes interact and integrate into the behavior of a whole organism — over time, across contexts, and from the molecular, moment-to-moment control of behavior up to its molar, aggregate patterns.

We pursue that question across a basic → translational → applied continuum: human operant experiments and artificial organisms that test how processes combine, then carrying that work into socially important domains — ethics, clinical and autism service delivery, substance use, baseball, and more. Running through all of it is a common methodological backbone: rigorous science, expanding data, and sophisticated analysis.

    How it fits together

    Four lines of work, one organism

    No single principle explains the behavior of a whole organism, and none of these lines stands alone. They feed each other across the basic–to–applied continuum.

    Basic

    Basic principles & their interactions

    Human operant experiments isolate how individual processes combine — the raw material everything else is built on.

    Basic

    Artificial organisms

    We test that understanding by building it: when a constructed system reproduces real behavior it sharpens the theory; when it breaks, it shows what's missing.

    Translational · Applied

    Applied & translational domains

    Validated combinations move into messier, socially important settings — ethics, clinical and autism services, substance use — where new constraints reveal which principles actually matter, and pose fresh basic questions.

    Backbone

    Methodological backbone

    Rigorous design, expanding data, and quantitative modeling cut across all of it — letting us see the interactions and carry findings from one level to the next.

    The throughline is integration: molecular to molar, basic to applied, and back again.
